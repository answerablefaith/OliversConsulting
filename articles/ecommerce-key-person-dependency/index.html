Founder/operator lessons

Your ecommerce operation should survive one person being away

A capable employee can hide a fragile process. The warning arrives when they take leave and orders, stock updates or reports begin to stall.

Published 22 July 2026By Henry OliverUK ecommerce & wholesale

The most dangerous spreadsheet in an ecommerce business is often the one that works. It works because one person knows which tab matters, which formula lies and which supplier needs a special fix. The business mistakes that knowledge for a process.

I have seen this pattern inside real multi-channel operations. A team looks efficient because the same experienced person catches every problem. In truth, the operation is borrowing reliability from that person.

Competence can hide operational debt

Good people build workarounds because customers and marketplaces will not wait for a systems project. That is sensible. The problem starts when the workaround becomes permanent but remains undocumented.

The expert remembers that Amazon stock needs a buffer, one wholesale customer uses old product codes and Friday’s supplier file has different columns. None of this appears in the written process. It lives in judgement, inbox history and muscle memory.

This creates two costs. The obvious cost is disruption when the person is away. The less visible cost is that the expert cannot move into higher-value work because the business needs them to keep rescuing routine tasks.

My rule: a process is not stable because one person can run it quickly. It is stable when another capable person can run it safely from the available records and rules.

The five-day absence test

Day-one workList every task that would stop or wait if the usual owner were absent tomorrow.
InputsCan another person find the correct files, inboxes, exports and system views without being told?
DecisionsAre price, stock, customer and approval rules written down, or does someone “just know”?
ExceptionsCan the backup see what failed, why it failed and what action is safe?
RecoveryAfter five days, can the team prove what ran, what changed and what still needs attention?

Score each line red, amber or green. Red means the task stops or becomes unsafe. Amber means it continues with delay or heavy checking. Green means another trained person can run it with a clear audit trail.

This is not a staff performance review. It is a process test. The aim is to remove dependence on memory without pretending every judgement can be automated.

Documentation alone will not fix it

A long procedure can fail as easily as no procedure. Screens change. Supplier formats move. Edge cases appear. A useful operating record must show the current input, the rule, the owner and the evidence that the task completed.

Start with the smallest useful artefacts:

  • a named source for each input;
  • a short decision table for prices, stock or customer rules;
  • a list of known exceptions and their owners;
  • a run log that shows what changed;
  • a clear point where the process must stop rather than guess.

These records make handover easier now. They also reveal which parts are good candidates for automation later.

Do not automate the person’s memory

A common mistake is to watch the expert work, copy the visible clicks and call that the specification. The clicks are not the process. The hidden decisions are.

Ask why a row is changed, why an order is held and why a stock value is trusted. Separate repeatable movement from commercial judgement. Then automate only the part with stable inputs, clear outputs and agreed rules.

This is why the first build should often expose uncertainty rather than hide it. A process that produces a short, useful review list is stronger than one that completes every row by making silent assumptions.

The owner should become less essential to routine work

The goal is not to make experienced staff replaceable. It is to stop wasting their experience on avoidable rescue work.

When the process is visible, the expert can improve rules, train others and deal with the few decisions that need context. The business gains resilience. The employee gains room to grow.

This also changes the automation business case. Saving ten hours matters. Removing a single point of failure can matter more, but do not invent a cash value for it. Record the processes protected, the recovery time and the amount of senior attention released.

What I would do this week

Choose one recurring process: supplier updates, marketplace stock, wholesale orders, invoices or the weekly report. Ask the usual owner to take no part in one controlled test run.

Let a trained colleague use the files and instructions that already exist. Record every question they must ask. Each question reveals missing data, an unclear rule or hidden ownership.

Fix those gaps before buying software. Then review the decision to add more ecommerce admin and the wider ROI case for controlled automation. The best build removes fragile dependence. It does not turn one person’s undocumented habits into permanent code.
