Your ecommerce operation should survive one person being away

Ecommerce key-person dependency appears when one employee carries the rules, access and workarounds that keep a critical process moving.

Published 22 July 2026Updated 22 July 2026By Henry OliverUK ecommerce & wholesale
Quick answer

Reduce ecommerce key-person dependency by running a controlled five-day absence test. Check whether another trained person can find the inputs, apply the rules, handle exceptions and prove what completed. Fix missing ownership and records first. Automate only the stable parts, with clear stop conditions and human review.

The most dangerous spreadsheet in an ecommerce business is often the one that works. It works because one person knows which tab matters, which formula lies and which supplier needs a special fix. The business mistakes that knowledge for a process.

I have seen this inside real multi-channel operations. A team can look efficient because the same experienced person catches every issue. In truth, the operation is borrowing reliability from that person.

How does key-person dependency appear in ecommerce?

It usually starts with sensible workarounds. Customers, suppliers and marketplaces will not wait for a systems project, so a capable employee creates a spreadsheet, inbox rule or manual check that keeps the day moving.

The risk grows when the workaround becomes permanent but the rules stay hidden. The expert remembers that Amazon stock needs a buffer, one wholesale customer uses old product codes and Friday’s supplier file has different columns. None of that appears in the written process.

That creates two costs. The obvious one is disruption during leave or illness. The quieter one is trapped capacity. The expert cannot move into better work because routine tasks still need their memory.

The same pattern often sits behind failed supplier price-list automation and unreliable Shopify, Amazon and eBay stock updates. The software is not always the weak point. The missing business rule is.

My rule: a process is not stable because one person can run it quickly. It is stable when another capable person can run it safely from the available records and rules.

The five-day absence test

Day-one workList every task that would stop, wait or become risky if the usual owner were absent tomorrow.
InputsCan a backup find the correct files, inboxes, exports and system views without asking where they are?
DecisionsAre price, stock, customer and approval rules written down, or does someone simply know them?
ExceptionsCan the backup see what failed, why it failed, who owns it and which action is safe?
RecoveryAfter five days, can the team prove what ran, what changed and what still needs attention?

Score each line red, amber or green. Red means the task stops or becomes unsafe. Amber means it continues with delay or heavy checking. Green means another trained person can run it and leave a clear audit trail.

This is a process test, not a staff performance review. UK government business continuity guidance stresses continuity of critical functions, effective recovery and testing whether plans work. A document that has never been exercised is only a theory.

What should be recorded before a handover?

A long procedure is not the answer. Screens change, supplier formats move and edge cases appear. The useful record is short enough to maintain and specific enough to make a decision.

  • Named input: the exact inbox, report, export or folder used for the task.
  • Decision table: the rule for price, stock, customer, tax or approval choices.
  • Exception list: known failure types, their owners and the next safe action.
  • Run log: the date, source file, result, changes made and reviewer.
  • Stop condition: the point where the process must pause instead of guessing.

ICAEW’s updated Spreadsheet Competency Framework notes that weak spreadsheet skills create time leakage and raise the risk of errors in data used for decisions. The answer is not to ban spreadsheets. It is to match the tool, controls and skill level to the job.

How do you automate without copying hidden habits?

Watching the expert click through a task is not enough. The visible clicks are only the surface. Ask why a row changes, why an order is held and why one stock value is trusted over another.

Then name the mechanism. A parsing rule can read a supplier CSV. A lookup table can translate old customer codes. A tolerance check can stop a margin change outside the agreed range. An exception queue can send unclear rows to the right owner.

That is a safer specification than “copy what Sarah does”. The first build should expose uncertainty rather than hide it. A short review list is better than a system that completes every row by making silent assumptions.

The same principle applies to weekly ecommerce reporting. Automate the exports, joins and checks. Keep the final commercial explanation with a person who understands the business.

How should the business case be measured?

Do not invent a cash value for resilience. Measure what you can prove: hours of senior rescue work, delayed tasks, recovery time, number of processes with no backup and the share of runs completed without questions.

Suppose a senior operations manager spends four hours each week answering routine questions and repairing handover gaps. At a loaded cost of £38 an hour, that is £152 a week. If clearer records and controlled automation return three hours, the capacity value is £114 a week, or £5,472 across 48 working weeks.

Use your own numbers and test them against the ecommerce automation ROI framework. The value is released capacity and lower recovery effort, not a promise that every saved hour becomes cash.

What should you do this week?

Choose one recurring process: supplier updates, marketplace stock, wholesale orders, invoices or the weekly report. Ask the usual owner to take no part in one controlled run.

Let a trained colleague use the files, access and instructions that already exist. Record every question they must ask. Each question points to a missing input, unclear rule, access problem or hidden owner.

Fix those gaps before buying software or adding another role. The article on checking the process before hiring ecommerce admin shows how to separate judgement from repeatable data movement. The right build reduces fragile dependence. It does not turn one person’s undocumented habits into permanent code.

FAQs

What is key-person dependency in ecommerce?

Ecommerce key-person dependency exists when a critical task relies on one person’s memory, access, judgement or workaround. The process becomes slow or unsafe when that person is unavailable.

How can an ecommerce business test key-person risk?

Run one recurring process without its usual owner for a controlled five-day period. Record every missing input, unclear rule, access problem, exception and recovery step.

Should key-person dependency always be solved with automation?

No. Fix unclear ownership, missing rules and weak records first. Automate stable data movement and checks, while commercial judgement and unusual exceptions stay with trained people.

What should be documented before automating an ecommerce process?

Record the source inputs, decision rules, owners, exception actions, stop conditions and evidence that each run completed. These records become the handover guide and build specification.
