How to stop re-keying wholesale orders into Sage or Xero

If someone reads each order and types the same details into the finance system, that person is acting as the integration. Fix the hand-off without removing the checks.

Published 10 July 2026Updated 17 July 2026By Henry OliverUK ecommerce & wholesale
Quick answer

Automate the hand-off, not the judgement. Read each order, map the customer and product codes, check prices, quantities, delivery details and totals, then create a draft record. Orders that pass can move forward. Anything unclear should stop with a reason and the source beside it.

Wholesale orders arrive by email, PDF, spreadsheet or portal. Someone finds the account, matches the customer’s product code to the internal SKU, types each line into Sage or Xero, and checks the total.

That person is acting as the integration. Re-keying also creates errors: a wrong pack size, old address, duplicate purchase order or price from the wrong list. A useful workflow removes the typing but keeps the checks.

Start with one real order

Follow a recent order from arrival to the final sales record. Write down each decision a person makes.

  • Which customer account does this order belong to?
  • Does the customer use your SKU or their own product code?
  • Which price list or discount rule applies?
  • Is the quantity valid for the pack size?
  • Does the delivery address match an approved location?
  • Has this purchase order already been entered?
  • Does the final total agree with the source?

This is the real specification. If two people would answer the same question differently, fix the rule before automating it.

Use a rules sheet before a build

For each important field, record the source, check, action and stop condition. A simple rules sheet is more useful than a long automation brief.

Customer

Match the name, account number or email domain to one approved account. Stop if there is no confident match.

Product

Translate customer product codes to internal SKUs with a controlled mapping table. Stop unknown or retired codes.

Price

Compare the order price with the agreed list or customer rule. Stop differences outside the approved tolerance.

Quantity

Check units, cases and pack sizes. Do not silently turn twelve cases into twelve units.

Duplicate

Check the customer and purchase order number before creating another record.

The rules sheet becomes the test plan and keeps the logic visible.

Draft first, post later

The safest first version prepares an order for review. It extracts the fields, applies the checks and creates a clean draft for the next system.

Do not post every order automatically. Demos show the happy path; live orders contain exceptions. The value comes from handling them well.

A stopped order is not a failed automation. It is proof that the control worked.

Show the source value, expected value and reason for each stop. “Price mismatch: ordered £8.40, expected £8.75” is useful. “Validation error” is not.

When drafts are reliable, decide which orders can move without review. Use the pass rate, exception types and cost of a bad order.

Measure more than time saved

Suppose 80 orders arrive each week and each takes six minutes to read, enter and check. That is eight hours a week. At a loaded staff cost of £25 an hour, the direct cost is £200 a week, or £9,600 across 48 working weeks.

If the workflow cuts average handling to one minute of review, effort falls to about 80 minutes. That releases more than six and a half hours each week.

Saved time only has value when it is used. It may help the same person chase debt, resolve customer issues or absorb growth without another admin hire.

Track four measures during a pilot:

  • average handling time per order;
  • percentage of orders that pass without changes;
  • number and type of exceptions caught; and
  • corrections, credits or queries caused by entry errors.

These numbers tell you whether to expand, change the rules or stop. They are more useful than claiming the workflow is “fully automated”.

Keep an audit trail

Every created order should link to the email, PDF, spreadsheet or portal record that started it. Store the source reference, values read, checks applied and any changes made by a person.

When a customer questions an order weeks later, you should be able to see what arrived, why it stopped or passed, and who approved an override.

Limit access to the inboxes, folders and systems needed. Test before live posting, and keep a clear way to pause the workflow when a source format changes.

Know when not to automate

Do not automate a process that changes on every order and lives in one person’s memory. If prices are agreed by phone, customer codes are inconsistent and delivery rules are unclear, automation will turn hidden confusion into faster confusion.

Low volume can make a custom build hard to justify. Ten simple orders a month may be better handled with a clean template and checklist. Automation is stronger when volume is steady, rules repeat or errors are costly.

What to bring to a process review

Bring 20 to 50 recent orders, including awkward examples. Bring the import template or list of fields entered into Sage or Xero, plus the customer, product and price data used for matching.

Mark the exceptions: customer product codes, old SKUs, special prices, split deliveries, missing fields and orders that need approval.

That is enough to test stability, estimate payback and decide what stays with a person. The goal is a controlled hand-off that removes repeated typing without weakening commercial checks.

FAQs

Can wholesale order entry into Sage or Xero be automated?

Yes, when customer, SKU and price rules can be checked. Unknown or risky values should stop for review.

Should every wholesale order post automatically?

No. Start with checked drafts. Allow automatic posting only after controls and exception handling work with real orders.

What should be checked automatically?

Check customer accounts, product codes, prices, pack sizes, duplicate orders, delivery details and source totals.

How should I judge the payback?

Measure volume, handling time, staff cost, pass rate and corrections. Compare the annual capacity released with the build and running cost.
