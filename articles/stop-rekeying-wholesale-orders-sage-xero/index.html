Wholesale and finance admin

How to stop re-keying wholesale orders into Sage or Xero

If someone reads each order and types the same details into the finance system, that person is acting as the integration. Fix the hand-off without removing the checks.

Published 10 July 2026Updated 17 July 2026By Henry OliverUK ecommerce & wholesale
Quick answer

Wholesale order entry into Sage or Xero can be automated when customer, product, price and quantity rules are clear. Extract the order data, match it against controlled records, create a checked draft and send exceptions to a person. Start with draft creation; only post automatically after real orders prove the controls.

Wholesale orders arrive by email, PDF, spreadsheet or customer portal. Someone finds the account, matches the customer’s product code to the internal SKU, types each line into Sage or Xero, and checks the total.

That person is acting as the integration. A useful workflow removes repeated entry while keeping commercial judgement with the team.

Start wholesale order entry automation with one real order

Follow a recent order from arrival to the final record. Write down each decision: customer account, product-code match, price rule, pack size, delivery address, duplicate purchase order and source total.

This is the real specification. If two experienced staff members would answer differently, fix the rule before automating it. Software cannot resolve an argument the business has never settled.

How should the workflow read email, PDF and spreadsheet orders?

Name the mechanism. A text PDF can be parsed with field rules or a customer template. A scanned document may need optical character recognition, usually called OCR. A spreadsheet can use a fixed column map. Portal orders may arrive through an export, attachment or another supported connection.

Then check each value. Use a lookup table to translate customer product codes to internal SKUs. Use account data for approved addresses. Use a tolerance check for prices. Use the customer and purchase order number together to find duplicates.

Reading a value is not the same as trusting it. The workflow should show where the value came from and why it passed.

Build a rules sheet before you commission the work

For each field, record the source, check, action and stop condition. A short rules sheet gives a developer something testable and gives the operations team something they can challenge.

Customer

Match one approved account. Stop if the name, number or sender is unclear.

Product

Translate customer codes with a controlled mapping table. Stop unknown or retired codes.

Price

Compare against the agreed customer rule. Stop differences outside the approved tolerance.

Quantity

Check units, cases and pack sizes. Never silently turn twelve cases into twelve units.

Duplicate

Check the customer and purchase order number before creating another draft.

Give every exception an owner. “Finance checks price differences” is better than sending all failures to a shared inbox.

Use three stages: extract, draft, then post

The safest first version extracts the order and prepares a review screen. The second creates a checked draft or import-ready record for the current Sage or Xero process. The third allows proven order types to move without review.

Do not jump straight to automatic posting. Live orders contain old codes, special prices, split deliveries and last-minute notes. Demos rarely do.

An exception queue should show the source, failed field, value read, expected value and reason. “Ordered £8.40; expected £8.75 under customer price list A” is useful. “Validation error” is not.

During a pilot, review every order. Separate bad source data, missing rules and extraction errors. Each needs a different fix.

How do you calculate the payback?

Suppose 65 orders arrive each week and each takes seven minutes to read, enter and check. That is about 7.6 hours. At a loaded staff cost of £27 an hour, the direct handling cost is about £205 a week, or £9,828 across 48 working weeks.

If a checked draft reduces average handling to 75 seconds, weekly effort falls to about 81 minutes. The team releases roughly 6.2 hours each week.

Do not treat every saved minute as cash. Decide what the capacity will do: chase debt, answer customers, correct catalogue data or absorb growth without another admin hire. Then compare the annual capacity value with the build and running cost.

Keep the source, audit trail and pause control

Every created record should link to the email, PDF, spreadsheet or portal record that started it. Store the values read, rules applied and any override made by a person.

Keep a clear pause control. When a customer changes its layout or sends a new format, the safe response is to stop and review, not guess.

When should wholesale order entry stay manual?

Do not automate a process that changes on every order and lives in one person’s memory. If prices are agreed by phone, product codes are inconsistent and nobody knows which delivery rule wins, map the process first.

Low volume can also make a custom build hard to justify. Ten simple orders a month may be better handled with a clean template and checklist. Start with a repeat customer that has a stable format and clear rules.

What should you bring to a process review?

Bring 20 to 50 recent orders, including awkward examples. Bring the current Sage or Xero fields, plus the customer, product and price data used for matching. Mark special prices, split deliveries and approvals.

FAQs

Can wholesale order entry into Sage or Xero be automated?

Yes, when customer, product, price and quantity rules can be checked. Unknown or risky values should stop for review instead of being posted automatically.

Do PDF orders need AI to be read?

No. Text PDFs can often be parsed with field rules or templates. Scanned documents may need OCR, followed by the same checks and review for uncertain values.

Should every wholesale order post automatically?

No. Start with checked drafts and an exception queue. Allow automatic posting only for proven order types where the cost of an error is understood.

How should I judge the payback?

Measure order volume, handling time, staff cost, pass rate, exceptions and corrections. Compare the annual capacity released with the build and running cost.
