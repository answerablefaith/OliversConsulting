How to reconcile Shopify payouts, orders and fees

Shopify payout reconciliation should explain every bank deposit without treating the deposit as sales. The process must separate trading activity, payment activity and cash settlement.

Published 20 July 2026Updated 20 July 2026By Henry OliverUK ecommerce & wholesale
Quick answer

Match each bank deposit to one Shopify Payments payout, then split that payout into charges, refunds, fees and adjustments. Check that transaction net totals equal the payout and that the payout equals the bank line. Apply only agreed accounting mappings. Send unclear differences to a named reviewer with the source beside them.

A Shopify deposit rarely matches the sales shown for the same date. Orders settle later, while refunds, disputes and fees reduce the cash received. Third-party gateways may follow another timetable.

The figures can all be correct while the comparison is wrong. Reconcile trading activity, payment activity and cash settlement as separate records.

What does Shopify payout reconciliation compare?

Keep four records separate because each answers a different question.

Trading activity

Orders, discounts, shipping, VAT, returns and refunds explain what was sold or reversed.

Payment activity

Charges, fees, disputes and adjustments explain how money moved through Shopify Payments.

Bank activity

The deposit confirms that a payout reached the bank account.

Accounting entries

Sales, VAT, fees and clearing balances record the commercial meaning of those movements.

Shopify says its payout reconciliation report explains Shopify Payments balance activity, transactions, fees and payouts. It is not a revenue statement. It also excludes third-party processors and payment methods, so PayPal, Klarna and other gateways need their own settlement check.

Which source files and fields should the workflow use?

Use the Shopify Payments transaction export for payout detail, the reconciliation report for balance movement, and the accounting system or bank feed for matching deposits.

Keep one row per balance transaction:

  • Payout data: payout ID, payout date, currency, status and transfer reference.
  • Transaction data: transaction date, type, order or refund reference and gateway.
  • Amounts: gross amount, fee and net amount.
  • Controls: source file, import time, match result, exception reason and reviewer.

Use the payout ID as the grouping key. A parsing rule reads the export, a lookup table maps transaction types to accounting codes, and a duplicate fingerprint blocks the same file from being posted twice.

Keep this control separate from the management pack. The same source data may feed an automated weekly ecommerce report, but payout reconciliation must still prove the bank movement at transaction level.

Which checks prove that a payout is complete?

Run two checks.

  1. Payout check: add the net amount of all transactions linked to the payout ID. The result should equal the stated payout total.
  2. Bank check: match the payout amount, currency and transfer reference to one bank deposit.

Use a small rounding tolerance only where currency rules require it. A broad tolerance can hide a missing refund or fee. Every difference needs a named cause.

Control rule: the bank deposit clears the payment balance. It is not the sales figure.

How should exceptions be handled?

Give every payout one result.

  • Pass: transaction totals equal the payout, and the payout matches one bank line.
  • Review: the amounts match, but the gateway, tax code or account mapping is new.
  • Stop: the payout is duplicated, incomplete, in an unexpected currency or cannot be tied to the bank.

The exception queue should show the payout ID, difference, likely cause, source link, owner and next action. “Does not match” sends the investigation back to finance. “£43.20 short; two refund transactions are missing from the import” gives someone a clear job.

Assign ownership before the pilot. Finance owns the accounting map, operations confirms order and refund references, and the workflow owner fixes import failures. Explained timing gaps need a status and expected clearing date, not repeated investigation.

How should Shopify payouts reach Xero?

Agree the posting design with the accountant before building it. A common pattern uses a Shopify Payments clearing account. Sales and VAT are recorded from trading activity. Refunds reverse the agreed values. Fees go to the selected expense account. The bank deposit transfers the payout balance into the bank account.

The Shopify integration by Xero can sync daily summaries or individual sales and match payouts to bank deposits. That may be enough for a simple store, so test the native route before paying for a custom build.

A custom workflow becomes useful when several stores, entities, currencies, gateways or approval rules must be joined into one exception process.

What does the payback look like?

Measure report exports, bank matching, fee checks, refund investigation, journal repair and review before approving a build.

Suppose the team handles 85 payouts each month. At nine minutes per payout, the task takes 12.75 hours. At a loaded staff cost of £31 an hour, that is about £395 a month.

If controlled matching reduces review to three minutes, the task falls to 4.25 hours. The team gets back 8.5 hours a month, worth about £264. A £3,200 build has a time-only payback of just over 12 months.

How should a pilot be run?

Start with one complete month, including refunds, a dispute, a bank holiday, more than one currency and a third-party gateway. Run the workflow beside the current process.

Track pass rate, exception reasons, review time and post-entry corrections. Allow automatic posting only after the rules work across real payout cycles, and keep a pause control for changed exports or new transaction types.

When is a custom workflow not worth building?

Use the native integration or a checklist when there is one store, one entity, one currency, stable gateways and low volume. A better month-end process may save more time than custom software.

Do not automate unsettled accounting policy. If staff disagree about gift cards, VAT, fees or refunds, settle the treatment first. The same process check applies before adding ecommerce admin headcount: fix the rules before adding cost.

What should the finished workflow deliver?

The finished process should collect payment and bank data, block duplicate imports, group transactions by payout ID, run both checks, apply approved mappings and create a short exception queue. Every posting should retain its source and reviewer history.

FAQs

Why does a Shopify payout not equal sales for the same day?

Order dates, payment dates and payout dates are different events. A payout can also include refunds, fees, adjustments and activity from several days, so it should be matched to the Shopify Payments ledger rather than a same-day sales total.

What data is needed for Shopify payout reconciliation?

Keep the payout ID, transaction date, transaction type, order or refund reference, gross amount, fee, net amount, currency, gateway and bank reference. These fields support both the payout-total check and the bank-deposit check.

Should Shopify payouts be posted as revenue?

A Shopify payout is a transfer of funds, not a clean revenue figure. Sales, VAT, refunds, fees and payment-clearing movements should follow the accounting treatment agreed with your accountant.

When is a custom Shopify payout workflow unnecessary?

Use the native integration or a checklist when the store has one entity, one currency, stable gateways and low exception volume. Fix unclear tax, refund, gift-card or fee rules before automating any posting.
